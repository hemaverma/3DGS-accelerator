Deep Dive · Stage 1.5 · The Gaussian Blobs

Everything About the Blobs

Five properties. Each one doing a specific job.

First Principles

Why a Gaussian? Why Not a Box, or a Sphere?

A Gaussian has a magical property: it's infinitely smooth and differentiable everywhere. That means gradients always flow cleanly through it during training — no sharp edges to cause instability. It also naturally fades to zero at its edges, so blobs blend into each other seamlessly.

Shape Comparison — Why Gaussians Win

Voxel grid (left) has sharp edges and bad gradients. Triangle mesh (center) needs topology. Gaussian (right) is smooth, differentiable, and blends naturally.

The 1D Gaussian — Intuition First

The bell curve. In 3D, this becomes an ellipsoid. The key parameters are μ (center) and σ (spread). High value at center, smoothly fading to zero at edges.

Property 01 / 05

Position — Where Is This Blob?

The simplest property. Every Gaussian has a center point μ = (x, y, z) in 3D world space. During training, this coordinate gets nudged via gradient descent to move the blob toward where it "should" be.

Think of it as the blob's address in 3D space. Millions of addresses, each slightly different.

Positions Build the Scene — Sparse to Dense

100 Gaussians → 10K → 1M. Same positions, same scene. Density reveals detail.

Property 02 / 05

Shape — The Most Powerful Property

This is what makes 3DGS exceptional. Each blob isn't just a sphere — it can be a thin flat disk, a long needle, a fat ball, or any ellipsoid in between. This is encoded as a covariance matrix, but we can think of it as two things: scale (how big in each direction) and rotation (which way is it pointing).

A flat disk Gaussian can represent a wall surface. A thin needle Gaussian can represent a wire or edge. This adaptive shape is why 3DGS can represent scenes so efficiently.

The Shape Zoo — What a Gaussian Can Look Like

Same Gaussian, different scale + rotation parameters. Each is optimal for a different part of a scene.

Scale = 3 Numbers, Rotation = 3 Numbers → 6 total shape params

Scale (sx, sy, sz) stretches the blob along each axis

Rotation orients the ellipsoid — crucial for matching surface geometry

Why Not Just Use Spheres? — Efficiency Matters

A flat wall needs ~1 large flat disk Gaussian vs ~200 spheres. Ellipsoids = massive efficiency gain.

Property 03 / 05

Opacity — How Much Does This Blob Block?

Every Gaussian has an opacity value α ∈ [0, 1]. Zero means totally transparent (invisible). One means fully solid. Most Gaussians end up somewhere in between.

This matters enormously for blending. Semi-transparent Gaussians let you represent fog, glass, hair, and fine details that a solid mesh never could.

Opacity Spectrum — From Ghost to Solid

α=0.05 (near invisible) → α=0.3 → α=0.6 → α=1.0 (fully opaque)

Opacity × Shape = Representing Fine Detail

Hair, fur, smoke, and foliage are all representable — impossible with hard surface meshes.

Property 04 / 05

Color — Not Just RGB. View-Dependent Color.

Here's the subtle genius: a Gaussian doesn't store a single RGB color. It stores spherical harmonic (SH) coefficients — a compact encoding that lets the color change based on the viewing angle.

This captures specular highlights, iridescence, and any surface that looks different from different angles — just like real materials. The degree of SH controls how much the color can vary.

View-Dependent Color — Same Blob, Different Angles

The same Gaussian appears blue from the left, teal from the front, green from the right — like a real specular surface.

Spherical Harmonics — Color as a Function on a Sphere

Degree 0 = constant color (just RGB). Degree 1 = simple gradient. Degree 3 = complex view-dependent shading. 3DGS uses up to degree 3 (48 color coefficients per Gaussian).

Putting It All Together

One Gaussian = 59 Numbers

Every single blob in the scene is fully described by 59 floating-point numbers. All of them are optimized simultaneously during training.

The Full Parameter Budget Per Gaussian

59 params × 3M Gaussians = ~177M numbers per scene. Each is independently learned.
